Modular arithmetic is something that you will most likely not need to use in an interview, but it's a simple concept with a big effect, so it's good to know just in case. Outside of interviews, it is an important concept for competitive programming in general and a few LeetCode problems. Sometimes, a problem will ask you to return an integer answer mod x . If you're on LeetCode, x is usually = 1,000,000,007. The reason for this is that the real answer could be massive, too large to store in a variable. In these problems, one thing you could do is calculate the actual massive answer, and then return ans % MOD at the end. This would be correct, but the problem is, dealing with extremely large numbers is very inefficient, since the cost of arithmetic operations like addition and subtraction cannot be ignored when the number of digits is large. The interesting thing about the modulo operator is that you can use it at every step and still get the same answer. For example, let's say you had MOD = 7 and wanted to find the product and sum of [11, 15, 26, 43, 62] . Normal operation for product: 11 * 15 * 26 * 43 * 62 = 11437140 . 11437140 % 7 = 1 . Normal operation for sum: 11 + 15 + 26 + 43 + 62 = 157 . 157 % 7 = 3 . Now, let's take the modulo at every operation. Product: 11 * 15 = 165 % 7 = 4 4 * 26 = 104 % 7 = 6 6 * 43 = 258 % 7 = 6 6 * 62 = 372 % 7 = 1 Sum: 11 + 15 = 26 % 7 = 5 5 + 26 = 31 % 7 = 3 3 + 43 = 46 % 7 = 4 4 + 62 = 66 % 7 = 3 As you can see, the answers are the same! In fact, the answer is the same no matter how early you stop or no matter how many more numbers you add. By using modular arithmetic, we make sure that we aren't storing any massive numbers or performing operations on massive numbers. If you had a huge input, let's say a million integers, then the product could get ridiculous, let's say . In a language without overflow like Python, if you wanted the product mod 7, then performing multiplication on the numbers from 0 - 6 is a lot faster than performing multiplication on numbers on the order of . In a language with overflow, then it's a good bet to keep the numbers you're working with smaller, regardless of speed. Example 1: 2233. Maximum Product After K Increments You are given an array of non-negative integers nums and an integer k . In one operation, you may choose any element from nums and increment it by 1 . Return the maximum product of nums after at most k operations, modulo . The optimal way to perform the increments is to greedily increment the smallest element at every operation. The mathematical explanation is as follows: You have x > y If you increment x , then (x + 1) * y = xy + y If you increment y , then x * (y + 1) = xy + x The second product is larger since x > y We know how to continuously find the smallest element - with a heap. Convert nums into a min heap, pop from it, add 1 to the result, then push it back. Do this k times, and at the end, multiply all the numbers together and return the answer. While this program technically gives a correct answer, it will run into Time Limit Exceeded or even produce a wrong answer depending on the language you use as the product gets too large for even a long or long long to handle. If we instead take the modulus at every operation, then it will pass as accepted: Example 2: 2575. Again, you are unlikely to use modular arithmetic in an interview, but in the small chance that you can, this knowledge is very important. In any scenario (even outside of an algorithm problem) where you could have potentially massive numbers, then you should think about modular arithmetic.
